Supermarket Entrance Counter

A digital logic circuit designed to detect and count people using an IR sensor and 7-segment displays.

Situation & Task

Commercial spaces like supermarkets require an efficient method to track the number of people entering, both for business analytics and for managing occupancy. The task was to design and build a cost-effective electronic counter that could automatically detect a person passing through an entrance and display the count numerically, eliminating the need for manual counting.

My Role & Responsibilities:

I was the sole designer and engineer for this project. My responsibilities included designing the entire digital logic circuit, selecting the appropriate ICs and components, simulating the circuit's behavior in Multisim to verify its functionality, and building and testing a physical prototype on a breadboard.

Action

I designed the system using fundamental electronic principles and components:

1. Sensor & Comparator Circuit

I used an IR transmitter and photodiode pair as the motion sensor. When the beam is broken, the change in voltage is fed into an LM358 op-amp configured as a comparator. This produces a clean digital pulse for the counting logic.

2. Digital Counting Logic

The pulse from the comparator clocks two cascaded 74LS90 decade counters. The first counter handles the "ones" digit, and its output clocks the second counter for the "tens" digit, allowing the system to count from 0 to 99.

3. Display Driver Circuit

The Binary-Coded Decimal (BCD) output from each 74LS90 counter was fed into a corresponding 74LS47 BCD-to-7-segment driver. This IC translates the binary count into the correct pattern to illuminate the segments of the two 7-segment LED displays.

Result

The project was a success, resulting in a functional prototype that accurately counted objects passing through its sensor range. The design was validated both in simulation and through physical testing.

Accurate Counting

The final breadboard prototype reliably detected interruptions of the IR beam and incremented the 7-segment displays correctly from 0 to 99.

Successful Simulation & Build

The physical circuit's performance matched the successful simulations conducted in Multisim, demonstrating a solid translation from design to a working hardware prototype.

Key Learnings

  • This project provided practical, hands-on experience with fundamental digital logic ICs, such as counters and display drivers, reinforcing theoretical knowledge.
  • I learned the importance of analog-to-digital signal conditioning by using an op-amp comparator to convert the analog sensor reading into a clean clock pulse suitable for digital logic.
  • I gained valuable experience in translating a circuit from a simulation environment (Multisim) to a physical breadboard prototype, including troubleshooting real-world issues that don't appear in simulation.
