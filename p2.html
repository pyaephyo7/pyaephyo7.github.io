Situation & Task

The inherent variability of wind makes forecasting power generation a significant challenge for grid stability and energy security. The objective of this study was to leverage machine learning to create a predictive model that could accurately forecast wind turbine power output using historical meteorological data.

My Role & Responsibilities:

I led this research project, handling all aspects from data acquisition to final analysis. My responsibilities included performing exploratory data analysis (EDA), selecting and implementing appropriate machine learning models, conducting feature engineering and hyperparameter tuning, and critically evaluating the performance of each model to identify the most effective forecasting solution.

Action

I followed a structured data science workflow to develop the predictive models:

1. Data Analysis & Preprocessing

I began with an in-depth exploratory data analysis (EDA) of hourly weather and power data from 2013-2016. Using visualizations like heatmaps and scatter plots, I identified key relationships. The data was then cleaned and normalized using `MinMaxScaler` to prepare it for modeling.

2. Model Selection & Training

I selected three regression models for comparison: Linear Regression (as a baseline), Decision Tree, and a more complex Random Forest model. The dataset was split 80/20 for training and validation.

3. Feature Engineering & Tuning

Feature importance analysis revealed that wind speed at 100m (`WS_100m`) was the most dominant predictor. I used Grid Search for hyperparameter tuning to optimize the Decision Tree and Random Forest models, focusing on parameters like tree depth and sample splits to prevent overfitting.

Feature importance analysis from the Random Forest model.

Result

The study successfully demonstrated that machine learning can significantly improve the accuracy of wind power forecasting. The Random Forest model emerged as the superior solution.

Superior Predictive Accuracy

The Random Forest model achieved the highest RÂ² score of 0.5549, outperforming both the Decision Tree (0.5157) and the Linear Regression baseline (0.4654).

Identified Key Predictor

The analysis definitively confirmed that wind speed at 100m (`WS_100m`) is the most critical variable for predicting power output, accounting for 78% of the model's predictions.

Key Learnings

  • This project underscored the power of ensemble methods like Random Forest to capture complex, non-linear relationships in environmental data where simpler models fall short.
  • I learned that rigorous feature selection is as crucial as model selection; identifying the dominant predictor (`WS_100m`) was key to achieving high accuracy.
  • The study highlighted the trade-off between model complexity and interpretability, providing valuable experience in selecting the right tool for a specific engineering problem. Future work could explore neural networks for even greater accuracy.
